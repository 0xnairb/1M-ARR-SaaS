## Problem Interview Guide

### Goal
Understand if the problem is real, severe, and worth solving.

### Who to Interview
- **Ideal Number**: 15-20 people
- **Who**: People in your target market
- **Where to Find Them**:
  - LinkedIn (search + outreach)
  - Reddit (relevant subreddits)
  - Online communities (Slack, Discord)
  - Industry forums
  - Your existing network
  - Twitter/X
  - Facebook groups

### How to Recruit
**Outreach Template**:
```
Subject: Quick question about [their job/industry]

Hi [Name],

I'm researching challenges that [target personas] face with [problem area].
I noticed you [specific detail about them] and thought you'd have valuable insights.

Would you be open to a quick 15-minute call? Happy to buy you a coffee/beer for your time.

Thanks,
[Your Name]
```

**Incentive Options**:
- $25 Amazon gift card
- Coffee/beer on you
- Free access to eventual product
- Just genuine interest (works better than you think)

### Interview Structure (15 minutes)

**Introduction (2 min)**
```
Thanks for your time! I'm researching [problem area] to better understand
how people like you handle [workflow/task].

There are no right or wrong answers - I'm just trying to learn.
Is it okay if I take notes?
```

**Discovery Questions (10 min)**

1. **Current State**
   - "Walk me through your current process for [task/workflow]?"
   - "How often do you do this?"
   - "Who else is involved?"

2. **Pain Points**
   - "What's most frustrating about that process?"
   - "Where does it break down?"
   - "What takes the most time?"

3. **Attempted Solutions**
   - "What have you tried to improve this?"
   - "What tools do you currently use?"
   - "Why didn't those work?"

4. **Impact**
   - "How much time does this cost you per week/month?"
   - "What's the cost of NOT solving this problem?"
   - "If this problem went away, what would that enable you to do?"

5. **Willingness to Pay**
   - "If there was a solution that [solved specific pain], what would that be worth to you?"
   - "How much do you currently spend trying to solve this?"
   - "What would you be willing to pay monthly for a solution?"

**Wrap-Up (3 min)**
```
This has been super helpful. Last question:

If I built something to solve [specific pain point they mentioned],
would you be interested in being an early tester?

[If yes] Great! Can I add you to my list and follow up when I have something to show?
```

## Solution Interview Guide

### Goal
Validate that your proposed solution resonates and people understand the value.

### Prerequisites
- âœ… Completed 15+ problem interviews
- âœ… Identified top 3 pain points
- âœ… Created solution mockup (Figma, slides, or Loom video)
- âœ… Have a proposed pricing range

### Who to Interview
- **Start with**: People from problem interviews who showed high interest
- **Expand to**: 10-15 new people in target market
- **Total**: 20-25 solution interviews

### What to Prepare

**1. Solution Mockup**
- Figma wireframes (10-15 screens)
- OR Slide deck with key screens
- OR Loom video walkthrough (3-5 min)
- Show the core workflow only

**2. Value Proposition Statement**
```
We help [target customer]
Achieve [outcome]
Without [pain point]
Unlike [alternative]
```

**Example**:
```
We help dental practice managers
Stay compliant with HIPAA requirements
Without hiring expensive consultants
Unlike generic compliance software that doesn't understand dental workflows
```

### Interview Structure (20 minutes)

**Introduction (2 min)**
```
Thanks for jumping on again! Last time we talked, you mentioned [specific pain point].

I've been working on a potential solution and wanted to get your honest feedback.
This is very early - so nothing is set in stone.

Can I show you what I'm thinking and get your thoughts?
```

**Solution Walkthrough (5 min)**
- Share screen / mockup
- Walk through core workflow
- Explain how it solves their specific pain
- **Shut up and let them react**

**Reaction Questions (10 min)**

1. **Initial Reaction**
   - "What's your first reaction?"
   - "Does this make sense?"
   - "What's confusing?"

2. **Value Perception**
   - "How would this change your current process?"
   - "What would you use this for?"
   - "What's missing?"

3. **Feature Prioritization**
   - "If I could only build 3 things, which 3 matter most?"
   - "What would you need to see before you'd use this?"
   - "What features could I skip for V1?"

4. **Pricing Validation**
   - "If this existed today, would you use it?"
   - "What would you expect to pay for something like this?"
   - [Present pricing] "How does $X/month sound?"
   - "Too high, too low, or about right?"

5. **Competitive Comparison**
   - "How does this compare to [existing tool]?"
   - "Why would you choose this over [alternative]?"
   - "What would make this a no-brainer?"

6. **Commitment Check**
   - "If I built this, would you be willing to pay for early access?"
   - "What would you need to see to commit to $X/month?"
   - "Would you be willing to prepay for a year at a discount?"

**Wrap-Up (3 min)**
```
This has been incredibly helpful.

[IF POSITIVE]
Based on what you've seen, would you be interested in being a beta customer
when I launch? I'm thinking $X/month, 50% off for the first year.

[IF NEGATIVE]
What would need to change for this to be valuable to you?
```

## Pricing Validation

### Pricing Research Methods

#### Method 1: Van Westendorp Price Sensitivity Meter

Ask these 4 questions:

1. "At what price would you consider this too expensive?" $_____
2. "At what price would you consider this a bargain?" $_____
3. "At what price would you begin to question the quality?" $_____
4. "At what price would you consider it expensive, but still worth considering?" $_____

**Analysis**:
- Plot responses
- Find "optimal price point" where most overlap
- This gives you a price range

#### Method 2: Tier Testing

Present 3 pricing tiers and ask:
- "Which tier would you choose?"
- "Why?"
- "What would make you upgrade to the next tier?"

**Example**:
```
[ ] Starter - $99/month
    - Feature A
    - Feature B
    - 5 users

[ ] Pro - $299/month
    - Everything in Starter
    - Feature C
    - Feature D
    - 15 users

[ ] Enterprise - $999/month
    - Everything in Pro
    - Feature E
    - Unlimited users
    - Priority support
```

#### Method 3: Value-Based Pricing Calculator

**Formula**:
```
1. Value delivered per month = $_____
2. Your price (10-30% of value) = $_____ - $_____
3. Competitive benchmark = $_____
4. Your position = [ ] Premium [ ] Market [ ] Budget
5. Recommended price = $_____
```

**Example**:
```
1. Save 20 hours/month at $100/hour = $2,000/month value
2. 10-30% of value = $200-600/month
3. Competitors charge $300-500/month
4. Position: Premium (better features)
5. Recommended: $399-499/month
```

### Pricing Validation Checklist

**Before finalizing pricing**:
- [ ] Interviewed 20+ prospects about pricing
- [ ] Tested 3 different price points
- [ ] Confirmed value delivered is 10x+ price
- [ ] Researched 5+ competitor pricing
- [ ] Calculated CAC and LTV at different prices
- [ ] Tested annual vs monthly preference
- [ ] Identified price sensitivity thresholds

**Validation Criteria**:
- âœ… <30% say "too expensive"
- âœ… 10%+ willing to pre-pay
- âœ… Price > $50/month (for B2B SaaS)
- âœ… Can articulate clear ROI

## MVP Scope Definition

### The Minimum Viable Product Framework

**MVP â‰  Minimum Features**
**MVP = Minimum *Viable* Product**

Viable = Delivers core value + creates wow moment

### MVP Scoping Exercise

**Step 1: List All Possible Features**

Brainstorm everything your product *could* do:
1. _________________________________
2. _________________________________
3. _________________________________
[Continue to 20-30 features]

**Step 2: Categorize Using MoSCoW**

**Must Have** (product is useless without):
- _________________________________
- _________________________________

**Should Have** (important, but not critical):
- _________________________________
- _________________________________

**Could Have** (nice to haves):
- _________________________________
- _________________________________

**Won't Have** (future versions):
- _________________________________
- _________________________________

**Step 3: Identify the Core Workflow**

The ONE workflow that delivers 80% of the value:

```
User has [problem]
â†’ User does [action 1]
â†’ Product does [thing]
â†’ User does [action 2]
â†’ User gets [outcome]
â†’ User sees [measurable result]
```

**Example (Compliance SaaS)**:
```
User has HIPAA compliance question
â†’ User asks question in plain English
â†’ Product analyzes question + current policies
â†’ User reviews recommendations
â†’ User implements recommendations
â†’ User gets compliance report
```

**Step 4: Define "Aha Moment"**

The moment when user realizes value:

"The first time a user _____________, they'll understand the value."

**Examples**:
- "...sees their compliance score improve"
- "...automates their first workflow"
- "...generates their first report"
- "...connects their tools and sees data flow"

**Step 5: Reduce to Absolute Minimum**

For each "Must Have" feature, ask:
- Can we do this manually at first? (Concierge MVP)
- Can we fake this with existing tools? (Wizard of Oz)
- Can we build 20% of this that delivers 80% of value?

**The Rule**: If you can cut it and still deliver the "aha moment," cut it.

### MVP Build Time Estimate

**Realistic Timeline**:
- Simple CRUD app: 4-6 weeks
- Integration/automation tool: 6-8 weeks
- Data/analytics platform: 8-12 weeks
- Complex workflow tool: 12-16 weeks

**Be Honest**:
- What you think: 4 weeks
- Reality: 2x that (8 weeks)
- Safe estimate: 3x your estimate (12 weeks)

### MVP Launch Checklist

**Before calling it "done"**:

**Core Functionality**:
- [ ] Core workflow works end-to-end
- [ ] User can sign up
- [ ] User can pay (Stripe integration)
- [ ] User can log in/out
- [ ] User can achieve "aha moment"

**Essential Features**:
- [ ] Basic error handling
- [ ] Data persistence (won't lose work)
- [ ] Email notifications (transactional)
- [ ] Password reset flow
- [ ] Settings/preferences

**Security Basics**:
- [ ] HTTPS enabled
- [ ] Passwords hashed
- [ ] SQL injection prevention
- [ ] XSS protection
- [ ] CSRF tokens

**Polish** (don't overdo this):
- [ ] Loading states
- [ ] Error messages (helpful, not cryptic)
- [ ] Mobile responsive (basic)
- [ ] Empty states

**Nice to Haves** (skip for MVP):
- âŒ Beautiful design
- âŒ Animations
- âŒ Dark mode
- âŒ Keyboard shortcuts

## Validation Anti-Patterns (What NOT to Do)

### âŒ Common Mistakes

**1. Talking to Friends/Family**
- They'll tell you what you want to hear
- They're not real customers
- False validation

**2. Asking Leading Questions**
- "Would you use a tool that solves X?" (They'll say yes)
- Instead: "How do you currently solve X?"

**3. Pitching Instead of Listening**
- You talk 80%, they talk 20% = Bad
- They talk 80%, you talk 20% = Good

**4. Only Talking to People Who Agree**
- Selection bias
- Interview skeptics too

**5. Building Before Validating**
- "If I build it, they will come" (They won't)
- Always validate first

**6. Ignoring Negative Feedback**
- "They just don't get it" (Red flag)
- Listen to objections carefully

**7. Confusing Politeness for Interest**
- "That's interesting" â‰  "I'll pay for that"
- Look for specific commitment

### âœ… Best Practices

**1. Ask Open-Ended Questions**
- "Tell me about..." > "Do you..."
- Let them guide the conversation

**2. Listen for Pain, Not Solutions**
- They'll tell you what they think they need
- Focus on the underlying pain

**3. Look for Intensity**
- "This is annoying" < "This costs us $10K/month"
- Severe pain = willingness to pay

**4. Test Willingness to Pay**
- Don't just ask "would you pay?"
- Ask "would you pre-pay now for early access?"

**5. Document Everything**
- Record interviews (with permission)
- Take detailed notes
- Note exact quotes
